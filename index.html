Endangered Languages

Languages are one of the most important expressions of cultural identity. When a language dies out, the cultural values and traditions of the former speakers are at risk of being erased from history. How common are endangered languages across the globe?

Scroll to find out more

UNESCO defines five main categories of language endangerment:

Vulnerable

Most children speak the language, but it may be restricted to certain domains (e.g., home).

Definitely Endangered

Children no longer learn the language as a 'mother tongue' in the home.

Severely Endangered

The language is spoken by grandparents and older generations; while the parent generation may understand it, they do not speak it to children or among themselves.

Critically Endangered

The youngest speakers are grandparents and older, and they speak the language partially and infrequently.

Extinct

The language has no speakers left.

What does all this mean?

Unfortunately, a large amount of languages today face the imminent threat of exctinction, and its only likely to get worse. It is difficult to put a finger on exactly one reason for why languages die - instead, it can be a result of many different factors such as linguistic evolution or colonisation. When these languages die, the cultures of their former speakers also risk dying out too. Therefore, it is important that we try to capture what remains of these languages before they become the remnants of a culture lost to time.
